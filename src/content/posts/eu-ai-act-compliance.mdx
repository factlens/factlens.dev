The EU AI Act exists because a lawyer cited six cases that never existed. Because a chatbot told a grieving customer to end their life. Because AI systems around the world are making promises, giving advice, and stating facts — and nobody knows which ones are true until something breaks.

Regulation is society's immune response to repeated injury. It's what happens when "trust us" stops working.

## What the Act actually asks

Article 15 doesn't ask whether your AI is intelligent. It asks whether you can prove it's accurate. Article 9 doesn't care about your architecture. It wants to know if you've identified what can go wrong and what you're doing about it. Article 72 doesn't want innovation stories. It wants evidence that you're still watching.

The bureaucratic language obscures a simple demand: show your work.

This is not optional. Penalties reach 35 million euros or 7% of global revenue. But more importantly, this is the new baseline for deploying AI in any serious context. Enterprise customers will ask. Procurement officers will require it. Insurance underwriters will condition coverage on it.

## Six documents

The Act requires six categories of compliance documentation for high-risk AI systems:

**Technical documentation dossier** (Article 11, Annex IV). The master document. System architecture, foundation model provenance, development process, version history, harmonised standards applied. This is what notified bodies request first.

**EU declaration of conformity** (Article 47, Annex V). The legal statement that your system meets requirements. Provider identification, risk classification, conformity assessment procedure.

**Accuracy and performance report** (Article 15). Ongoing quantitative evidence. Accuracy metrics, score distributions, performance trends, error classification, robustness assessment. Not a one-time snapshot — continuous documentation.

**Risk management record** (Article 9). Systematic risk identification and treatment. Risk register, mitigation measures, fundamental rights impact assessment, bias assessment, ongoing monitoring.

**Data governance documentation** (Article 10). How you handle data. Collection practices, preparation operations, representativeness assessment, bias examination, data quality management, GDPR Article 9 assessment for special categories.

**Post-market monitoring report** (Article 72). What happens after deployment. Performance data analysis, incident register, corrective actions, user feedback, risk reassessment.

## Regulatory foundation

The six documents map to specific regulatory requirements:

- EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689): Articles 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 17, 47, 72, Annexes IV and V
- ISO/IEC 42001:2023 (AI Management System): Clauses 6.1, 7.5, 8.2, Annex A
- NIST AI RMF 1.0: GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE functions
