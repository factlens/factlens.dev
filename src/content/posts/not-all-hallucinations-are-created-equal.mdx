## The word is wrong

Everyone uses the same word — "hallucination" — for every time an AI gets something wrong. A chatbot ignores the document you gave it. A model invents a university that doesn't exist. A language model states that the Treaty of Versailles was signed in 1920. All three get filed under the same label.

This is a problem. Not a semantic one. A practical one. Because lumping them together implies they have the same cause, the same fix, and the same detection method. They don't.

In our paper [A Geometric Taxonomy of Hallucinations in LLMs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.13224), we propose splitting "hallucination" into three distinct failure modes — each with a different geometric signature in embedding space. Two can be detected automatically. One cannot. Knowing which is which changes how you build and how you deploy.

## Three failures, not one

**Type I: Unfaithfulness.** You give the model a document. You ask a question about that document. The model ignores the document and generates something plausible from its training data instead. The source material is right there, and the model doesn't engage with it.

This is the most operationally common failure in retrieval-augmented systems. It is also the most detectable, because when a model engages with provided context, there is a measurable geometric trace: the response moves toward the source material in embedding space. When it doesn't, the trace is absent. The Semantic Grounding Index (SGI) measures exactly this — a ratio of angles on the unit hypersphere that captures whether the model did the work of engaging with the context or whether it orbited the question without ever visiting the evidence.

**Type II: Confabulation.** No source document is involved. The model generates content that is semantically foreign to the domain — invented institutions, fabricated mechanisms, fictional terminology. Not wrong details within a correct frame. Entirely new fabrications that shouldn't be there.

This is what most people picture when they hear "hallucination." And it has a distinct geometric signature: the displacement that confabulated content creates in embedding space points in a different direction than displacements created by grounded content. The Directional Grounding Index (DGI) measures this alignment. With domain-specific calibration, it achieves AUROC between 0.76 and 0.99.

**Type III: Factual error within a correct frame.** The model says the Treaty of Versailles was signed in 1920. The vocabulary is right. The syntax is right. The conceptual frame is right. The date is wrong.

This is where things get honest. Type III errors show 0.478 AUROC in our experiments — indistinguishable from a coin flip. We cannot detect them. And we can explain why.

## Why Type III is architecturally undetectable

Embeddings encode patterns of word co-occurrence. They capture what words tend to appear near other words. They do not encode whether a statement corresponds to external reality.

Consider two sentences: "The Treaty of Versailles was signed in 1919" and "The Treaty of Versailles was signed in 1920." The same vocabulary. The same syntax. The same distributional context. In embedding space, these two sentences occupy almost identical regions — because embeddings have no mechanism for knowing which date is factually correct.

This is not a flaw in our method. It is a property of how embeddings are constructed. Any detection system that relies on embedding geometry — ours included — will hit this wall. Statements that differ only in their correspondence to external facts, while sharing the same distributional profile, are geometrically indistinguishable.

We report this as a finding, not as an apology.

## The benchmark surprise

The most unexpected result from the paper concerns standard hallucination benchmarks.

On benchmarks where hallucinations are generated by language models (the standard evaluation approach), detection is domain-local. AUROC ranges from 0.76 to 0.99 within a domain but collapses to 0.50 — chance level — across domains. The discriminative directions between domains are approximately orthogonal, with a mean cosine similarity of -0.07.

But when we tested against human-crafted confabulations — invented institutions, redefined terminology, fabricated mechanisms created by humans rather than generated by a language model — a single global direction achieved 0.96 AUROC with only 3.8% degradation across domains.

The same method. Radically different results. Why?

Because benchmarks are measuring the wrong thing. When you prompt a language model to fabricate, it fabricates in a particular way — with stylistic artefacts that are specific to how that model writes when asked to lie. A detector trained on these benchmarks learns to recognise those stylistic artefacts, not grounding failures. It learns the accent, not the meaning.

Human-crafted confabulations don't carry those stylistic artefacts. They represent genuine semantic drift — content that shouldn't be there, regardless of how it was written. That is what an actual hallucination detector needs to find.

## What this means in practice

If you're deploying a RAG system, Type I is your primary exposure. Your users provide context, your model should use it. SGI tells you whether it did.

If you're deploying a conversational AI without source documents, Type II is what you need to watch. Your model is generating freely, and you need to know when it starts inventing things that don't belong. DGI, calibrated to your domain, detects this.

Type III — a factual error wearing the right vocabulary — requires a different class of tools. External fact-checking, knowledge bases, human review. Geometric methods cannot solve it. Anyone who claims otherwise has either not tested carefully or not understood the mathematics.

The three types require three different responses. Calling them all "hallucination" obscures this. A compliance officer who hears "we detect hallucinations" needs to know which kinds. A developer who integrates a detection tool needs to know what it can and cannot catch. Precision in taxonomy is precision in engineering.

## The geometry underneath

The deeper finding is structural. Detection within a domain works because each domain develops its own consistent grounding geometry during the training of the embedding model. Similar questions relate to similar answers in domain-specific ways. Detection across domains fails because these domain geometries are incommensurable — they develop independently and there is no mechanism to align them.

This is the structure of a fiber bundle: every local neighbourhood has a coherent coordinate system, but there is no global coordinate system that works everywhere. Detection requires calibration to the local geometry. Universal detection — a single threshold, a single direction that works across all domains — is not a product gap. It is a mathematical impossibility given how embedding spaces are constructed.

This constraint is what makes domain-specific calibration valuable. It cannot be bypassed. It must be done per domain, per deployment.
